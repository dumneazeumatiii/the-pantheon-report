The illusion of certainty: How can the Geopolitics around Rome's Fall in 476 AD connect to today's falling giants

Today's article was published by Michael Dyonses, one of the most well-known historians in the U.K. as he has a doctorate in Roman History and teached in many universities. In today's article he talks about the Fall of the Roman Empire in 476 AD and how their process is a mirror to the problems of today's declining powers. His article has been criticized and appreciated widely since it has been published. Here is his text on the past and current geopolitics of these falling giants and how deep their connection may really be.

1. The Fall of the Roman Empire

1.1 Diocletian's Rule

Rome did not fall. That phrase can be one of the most misleading pieces of historical writing. What actually did happen for the Romans to finally fall was a long process of framework failure across about two centuries ever since the early 300s, during which most people did not believe that their great and prosperous empire could ever fall. But they will eventually fall, as their expensive to keep borders, growing military of outsiders, and Diocletian's split, will show the cracks of the Roman system, as the wealthy senatorial class continued to grow ever-corrupt.

By 285 AD their borders stretched from northern Britain to Mesopotamia, a very expensive one to keep. Between 235 and 284 AD there were about over twenty men claiming the imperial title, and most of them died in a series of violent events and assassinations. Diocletian came to power in 284 AD and his reform, introducing a Tetrarchy, divided the country between four rulers. It was a functioning response, but it also acknowledged that the empire was no longer really governable as a single unit.

The split into East and West created a very uneven balance. The East kept Alexandria, Antioch, the trade routes to the outside world like to Persia or India, and dense populations contributing significant taxation income to the economy. The West got the short end of the stick: extensive borders along the Rhine and the Danube, a colossal military relative to the economy supporting it, fewer important cities, and a smaller tax base. These made it quite impossible to fully resolve the falling West and its borders, as what followed is truly a testament to how the Roman invincibility did fade.

1.2 Disastrous Migrations and the Border Crisis in the West

The Huns arrived in the Pontic steppe in early 370 AD and their movements established a domino effect across the whole European continent, pushing many peoples west into Roman territory. The Visigoths for example, expelled from their former lands, sent a delegation to Emperor Valens in 376 AD requesting permission to cross the Danube and settle inside the Roman lands. Valens agreed. Sure, maybe this was not a bad thing, but the execution was truly horrendous.

Roman officials managing the new populations were very corrupt. Weapons were confiscated and sold. Food was demanded in exchange for spoiled provisions. The people who came looking for safety were exploited by the officials supposed to properly integrate them into society. They revolted, as it is human to do so. The revolt spread faster than anyone anticipated, and a managed migration became a military crisis in the space of two years.

1.3 How did the "immortal" Roman System Break

The senatorial class owned vast agricultural estates across the territories of Italy, North Africa, Spain, and Gaul, and had the political connections to resist taxation. The burden fell on smaller landowners and traders with far less of a capacity in both politics and class to absorb it. The treasury ran habitually short. Successive emperors reduced the silver content of Roman coins, triggering serious inflation and eroding confidence in the currency. Real wages for ordinary people collapsed.

With no real revenue to pay soldiers, the state outsourced its military to foederati, those were outsider Germanic tribal groups loyal actually to their own commanders and not to Rome. When the moment came, the army defending the Western Empire had no particular reason to, as their loyalties were to their actual true home.

1.4 A Deconstruction lasting 100 Years

The Western Empire did not collapse in one moment. It deteriorated across a century through a sequence of military defeats, failing reforms, and border crises. Here is a simple chronology of what happened in the span of a century with the Western Roman Empire, until their actual fall.

376 AD: Massive Gothic crossings overwhelmed the local Roman infrastructure.

378 AD: The Battle of Adrianople, one of the most disastrous defeats in Roman history, with Emperor Valens getting killed by the Goths. Even with 10,000 more troops than the 20,000 Gothic rebels, they still ended up getting totally crushed.

410 AD: Alaric the king of the Visigoths sacks Rome. The city stood in Roman hands for over 800 years, this is the first time they lost it to an external force. It was a shock to the population at that time, they believed that Rome was impenetrable.

455 AD: The Vandals led by King Gaiseric sack Rome a second time following the murder of Emperor Valentinian III and Petronius Maximus seizing the throne. Maximus broke a treaty by marrying Valentian's widow and forcing her daughter Eudocia to marry his own son, she was promised actually and betrothed to Gaiseric's son. This prompted the invasion, which would lead to the origin of the word "vandalism".

476 AD: Odoacer, the Germanic military leader, deposes the last Western emperor, Romulus Augustulus, and sends the imperial regalia to Constantinople, proclaiming himself as King of Italy. He did not execute Romulus Augustulus, but instead exiled him, while he established the first "barbaric" kingdom of Italy.

2. The Recurring Constant: The Fall of Empires

2.1 The Certainty of Permanence

Every major power at its peak tends to believe it will last forever. Rome did survive many civil wars, plagues and even military annihilation before its actual fall. The ruling class thought that the system was fundamentally impossible to break, themselves creating the myth of Roma Aeterna which translates to Eternal Rome, while the state propaganda surely did work through many works of poetry and political moves. The problem is that being great before is a poor predictor of future survival once the conditions change. The belief in permanence persisted long after the conditions to make the total fracture of the Western Roman Empire possible even appeared.

2.2 The Schematic of Imperial Decline

In his book, the recognised author Joseph Tainter argued that civilisations grow by complexity the more they continue to exist. For example: more bureaucracy, more specialisation, larger militaries. Each addition is important but adds more burden as well. At some point the returns of the system break down and the complexity costs more than it produces. The population disengages, institutions lose legitimacy, wealth concentrates at the top. The middle bears an increasing share of the burden as the wealthy class decides to shelter themselves from the economic turmoil. Whenever a disastrous external factor appears, the system just breaks.

2.3 The Transition to Polycentric Fracture

When a dominant power weakens, the world does not become more stable. The rules enforced by that power stop being enforced. Regional actors start testing what they can get away with. Smaller conflicts escalate because there is no longer anyone with the interest and capacity to stop them. The Mediterranean after 476 AD was certainly more violent and unstable for most people. The stability Rome provided had a cost that only became visible after its fall.

3. The Hegemonies of the current West

3.1 The United Kingdom

The prime example for a more modern declining power: once Britain controlled roughly a quarter of the earth's land surface and population in 1900. World War Two left it victorious but literally bankrupt, they became dependent on American financial support and incapable of projecting imperial power at the same scale, as the British Raj decided to leave the crown in 1947 in a series of multiple events that led to it. Decolonisation followed not as a choice but as an acknowledgement of limits mostly.

In today's time, following Keir Starmer's resignation as a P.M. amid historically low approval ratings, Britain is now on its seventh prime minister in ten years. That rate of leadership turnover in a system designed for stability reflects the inheritance of difficult structural problems, a very much similar pattern to Rome's revolving-door emperors of the late imperial period.

3.2 The United States of America and the Challenges of Today

America's structural problems are harder to see from the inside because the country remains powerful in absolute terms especially outside, with it having the biggest military and presence in many of the NATO countries as a responder force against potential conflicts. But current military strength is not the same as a stable structure with actual equilibrium. Wealth concentration is at its highest since the 1920s, the share of national income going to the top percentile has roughly doubled since 1980, real wages for the bottom half have barely moved in forty years. The wealthy class grows forever while the middle and the bottom are just left to rot.

The countries watching this giant fall, following historic lows of approval rates of current leadership and a split political system between two forces, can afford to just sit and stay patient while the U.S. falls to its own problems. It is imperative that they take monumental actions towards correcting the failures of today, however that is mostly far-fetched as the administration is perhaps more occupied with posting AI content and edits on social media than enforcing real policy for people's lives and to further improve education.

4. The Comparison and Final Conclusion

The comparison between Rome and the modern West should not be overstated. Sure maybe the contexts are different, the period is different, but most people decide to not acknowledge the fact that it is impossible for a hegemony to continue, especially if they run on a highly corrupt system, where in reality, money buys states, votes, and even people. Maybe it is all about money in the end for some people. But nevertheless, it is just a speculation, and hopefully for the sake of stability, the current crises can be ended through honest work by the powers of this world.

What is important is that the underlying conditions recur. Inequality that erodes institutional legitimacy. Military commitments that outrun national resources. Political leadership focused on crisis management rather than structural repair. A governing class that believes in its own permanence past the point where that belief is justified. These patterns appeared initially in Rome, and continued through a series of Empires from the Byzantines which eventually did fall to the Ottoman forces, marking the end of the Eastern Roman Empire, and to the colonial powers such as France and the U.K. who have yet to find political stability in today's time, as well as the U.S. lately, with many crises started, and so many left to manage, while the foundation itself breaks down in time.

The Western Roman Empire's collapse could have been averted perhaps. Better decisions at key events could have produced different outcomes. The same remains true today, the trajectory of a nation is not a concluded destiny, but trajectories matter, and the ones currently visible in the major Western powers do not point in comfortable directions. Whether that changes depends on decisions being made now. History does not make those decisions. People do.
